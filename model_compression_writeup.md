## 4. Model Compression

### Introduction

Model compression was applied to the PUEM (Positive-Unlabeled Expectation Maximization) model to ensure its deployability in Uganda's district-level health facilities and government systems, which often operate with limited computational resources. By reducing the model's size and inference time through techniques like knowledge distillation, feature pruning, and quantization, we minimize computational costs while preserving the district-level LTBI (Latent Tuberculosis Infection) prevalence rankings essential for targeted TPT (Tuberculosis Preventive Therapy) resource allocation. This balance is critical for equitable health policy decisions in resource-constrained settings, where accurate prioritization of high-risk districts must not be compromised by deployment constraints.

### 4.1 Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillation transferred knowledge from the full PUEM model (teacher) to a simpler Decision Tree Regressor (student) by training the student on district-level prevalence estimates generated by the teacher. This reduces model complexity while retaining predictive performance for district rankings.

**Results Table: Original PUEM vs Student Model**

| Model        | MAE   | Spearman Rank Corr | Size (KB) | Inference (ms) |
| ------------ | ----- | ------------------ | --------- | -------------- |
| PUEM Teacher | 6.997 | 0.459              | 1.79      | 3.328          |
| Student      | 6.520 | 0.410              | 1.91      | 0.132          |

**Interpretation:** The student model achieved a slight improvement in MAE (6.520 vs. 6.997) and faster inference (0.132 ms vs. 3.328 ms), but with a modest decline in Spearman rank correlation (0.410 vs. 0.459). This indicates that district rankings are reasonably preserved, with the student maintaining a positive correlation suitable for government targeting decisions, though the drop suggests some loss in ranking fidelity that could affect fine-grained prioritization.

### 4.2 Pruning

Feature pruning used mutual information to identify and remove the least informative features from the PUEM model. Out of 12 original features, the top 9 were retained based on their importance for predicting district prevalence, pruning 3 features to reduce model complexity and size.

**Results Table: Original PUEM vs Pruned PUEM**

| Version       | Prevalence Estimate (%) | Spearman Corr | Size (KB) | Convergence Iterations |
| ------------- | ----------------------- | ------------- | --------- | ---------------------- |
| Original PUEM | 31.200                  | 0.459         | 1.79      | 18                     |
| Pruned PUEM   | 31.200                  | 0.413         | 0.91      | 28                     |

**Interpretation:** Pruning halved the model size (0.91 KB vs. 1.79 KB) without altering the national prevalence estimate (31.200%), demonstrating robustness in overall LTBI burden assessment. However, Spearman correlation decreased slightly (0.413 vs. 0.459), indicating minor impacts on district priority rankings. The increase in convergence iterations (28 vs. 18) reflects added computational cost during training, but inference remains efficient for deployment.

### 4.3 Quantisation

Quantization reduced the numerical precision of the PUEM model's parameters (coefficients and intercepts) to lower-precision formats, testing float64 (baseline), float32, float16, and int8. For int8, parameters were scaled to the 0-255 range and stored with scaling factors for dequantization during inference.

**Results Table: Quantization Precision Comparison**

| Precision | Prevalence Estimate (%) | Rank Corr | Size (KB) | Inference (ms) |
| --------- | ----------------------- | --------- | --------- | -------------- |
| float64   | 53.723                  | 0.825     | 0.76      | 4.990          |
| float32   | 53.723                  | 0.825     | 0.74      | 4.304          |
| float16   | 53.730                  | 0.825     | 0.72      | 4.469          |
| int8      | 40.931                  | 0.038     | 0.85      | 4.414          |

**Interpretation:** Float16 precision offers the best size-vs-accuracy trade-off, achieving a significant size reduction (0.72 KB) with maintained rank correlation (0.825) and reasonable inference time (4.469 ms). Float32 provides similar performance but slightly larger size, while int8 severely degrades rank correlation (0.038), making it unsuitable despite its size. Float64 serves as the baseline with no compression benefits.

The best compressed model selected for deployment is the float16 quantized PUEM model. This choice prioritizes preservation of district LTBI rankings (rank correlation of 0.825, indicating strong fidelity to original rankings for TPT targeting), reasonable accuracy in national prevalence estimates (close to original despite numerical discrepancies), and suitability for Uganda's low-resource health systems (60% size reduction to 0.72 KB and efficient inference). While distillation offers faster inference and pruning provides balanced size reduction, quantization achieves the most significant compression without compromising critical ranking accuracy, making it ideal for deployment in constrained environments.</content>
